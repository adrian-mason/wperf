# ADR-012: "No False Negatives" Guarantee Disposition

- **Status:** Accepted
- **Date:** 2026-03-17

## Problem

The original wPerf design asserted that the Cascade redistribution algorithm "will definitely converge to the true global bottleneck and will not produce false negatives." This claim, presented as a mathematical certainty, served as a foundational correctness guarantee for the entire system. However, the claim was challenged in the first technical review, and the challenge was never substantively addressed across 22 subsequent documents. The project must decide whether to maintain this guarantee, formally retract it, or replace it with an honest characterization of coverage.

The disposition of this claim has direct consequences for how the tool is positioned to users, what test assertions are appropriate, and whether the system's documentation makes promises the implementation cannot keep.

## Options Considered

### Option A: Maintain the Mathematical Certainty Claim

Continue to assert that the Cascade redistribution algorithm guarantees zero false negatives -- that every true global bottleneck will be identified.

**Pros:**
- Strongest possible correctness claim, differentiating wPerf from all competing tools
- Aligns with the theoretical framework of the OSDI'18 paper, which proves that Knot detection is sound under idealized conditions
- Simpler mental model for users: "if wPerf says there is no bottleneck, there is no bottleneck"

**Cons:**
- The claim was challenged with three specific breaking conditions and never defended
- Real production systems routinely violate the conditions required for the claim to hold
- Maintaining an indefensible claim erodes credibility when users encounter false negatives in practice
- Creates legal and professional liability if the tool is used for SLA or incident-response decisions based on the guarantee

### Option B: Explicitly Retract and Replace with Pragmatic Assurance

Formally retract the mathematical certainty claim. Replace it with a precise characterization of what the tool guarantees, what assumptions must hold, and what failure modes exist.

**Pros:**
- Honest characterization builds long-term trust
- Forces the documentation and test assertions to reflect actual system capabilities
- Directs engineering effort toward quantifying and minimizing false negative probability rather than ignoring it
- Enables users to make informed decisions about when to trust the tool's output

**Cons:**
- Weaker marketing position compared to an absolute guarantee
- Requires additional engineering effort to quantify coverage gaps and communicate them
- Users must understand caveats rather than relying on a simple binary promise

## Analysis

### The Claim's Full Lifecycle Through the Spec Chain

The "no false negatives" claim was introduced in the original design document as a mathematical certainty: the Cascade redistribution algorithm, combined with weight-threshold pruning, "will definitely converge to the true global bottleneck and will not produce false negatives (False Negative)." The argument rested on the assertion that redistributed edge weights precisely equal the theoretical upper bound of throughput improvement achievable by optimizing that edge, and that pruning therefore cannot remove any edge on the critical path.

The first technical design review (finding M-8) directly challenged this claim, identifying three specific conditions under which the guarantee breaks:

1. **Event loss:** The assertion depends on "precise time-overlap determination (affected by collection granularity)" -- if the BPF ring buffer overflows or events are dropped, overlap calculations become inaccurate and the redistribution may fail to propagate weight to the true bottleneck edge.

2. **Clock skew:** The assertion depends on "all relevant wakeup events being captured" -- but `bpf_ktime_get_ns()` uses `ktime_get_mono_fast_ns()`, which the kernel source code explicitly states is "not guaranteed to be monotonic across an update" during NTP/PTP clock slope adjustments. Timestamp inversion can cause the event correlation state machine to mismatch switch and wakeup events.

3. **Sampling gaps:** The assertion depends on the false-wakeup filter (events under 50 microseconds on the same futex address) not filtering out genuine short-duration bottleneck edges. In systems with high-frequency lock contention (thousands of 10-50 microsecond waits), the filter may aggregate or discard events that collectively represent the dominant bottleneck.

The review concluded that the claim should be weakened to: "under the premise of complete event collection, Cascade redistribution guarantees that critical path weights increase monotonically."

### The Challenge Was Ignored Twice

The response to the design review addressed only 13 of the original 32 review findings, silently dropping 19 items (a 59.4% omission rate). M-8 was among the dropped items. A follow-up audit explicitly tracked this omission, marking M-8 as "still applicable." The next response document again failed to address it. A subsequent audit recorded: "M-8: ignored."

Eventually, the wording shifted silently. In a later document, the language changed from "will not produce false negatives" to "completely cleaned up," which is a different and weaker claim. By the final implementation specification, the claim had disappeared entirely -- no false-negative guarantee of any kind remained. No replacement guarantee was offered. The absence was not called out or explained.

### This Is an Instance of a Systemic Pattern

The "no false negatives" claim follows a pattern identified across the 22-document spec chain that the pre-execution readiness audit termed "authority inflation": claims are introduced with unwarranted certainty, challenged, and then either silently weakened or quietly removed rather than formally retracted with an explanation.

A parallel case demonstrates the same pattern: the SCC Max-weight heuristic was initially presented as a mathematical necessity ("MUST take MAX"). A subsequent review proved the mathematical argument was flawed ("partially accept -- mathematical argument is flawed"). The final specification downgraded it to "a sorting heuristic, not a precise mathematical value." Like the false-negative claim, the downgrade happened without a formal retraction or explanation of what changed and why.

The pattern is dangerous because it leaves the spec chain's historical record ambiguous. A reader encountering only the original design document would believe the no-false-negatives guarantee is foundational. A reader encountering only the final specification would not know the guarantee ever existed or was retracted. Neither reader has the full picture.

### The Three Breaking Conditions Are Real in Production

Each of the three conditions identified in the original review challenge maps to a concrete production scenario:

**Ring buffer overflow (event loss):** Under high scheduling pressure (e.g., 10,000+ context switches per second per CPU on a 64-core machine), the BPF ring buffer can overflow. The archived project's BPF code contains a `drop_counter` that increments when `bpf_ringbuf_reserve` returns NULL, confirming that overflow occurs in practice. When events are dropped, the Cascade engine operates on an incomplete Wait-For Graph. An edge representing the true bottleneck may have insufficient weight because some of its constituent wait events were lost, causing it to fall below the pruning threshold.

**NTP adjustments (clock skew):** The final implementation specification acknowledges this directly: "`ktime_get_mono_fast_ns()` -- the kernel documentation explicitly states this interface is 'not guaranteed to be monotonic across an update' during NTP/PTP clock slope updates." The specification's mitigation is that modern x86 hardware with `constant_tsc` + `nonstop_tsc` limits the inversion magnitude to nanosecond-to-low-microsecond scale, which is below the 50-microsecond false-wakeup filter threshold. This is a pragmatic engineering argument, not a mathematical guarantee. It fails on architectures without synchronized TSC (some ARM platforms, virtualized environments with imprecise TSC emulation).

**Context-switch sampling granularity:** The system hooks `sched_switch` and `sched_wakeup` tracepoints, which fire on every context switch -- but only for transitions that pass through the scheduler. Cooperative yielding, user-space spinlocks (which burn CPU without entering the kernel), and hardware interrupts that preempt a thread for sub-microsecond durations are not captured. A bottleneck that manifests as user-space spinning rather than kernel-mediated blocking is invisible to the Wait-For Graph and constitutes a genuine false negative.

### Quantified Evidence of the Claim's Fragility

The archived project's empirical data provides direct evidence. The S-curve collapse -- where the fix rate jumped from near-0% to 88% in the final days -- included 8 cascade-specific fixes. The `cascade.rs` file was modified 44 times. Among the fixes was a commit noting that `merge_thread_groups` executing before cascade caused 66.7% weight loss. Another fixed corrupted timestamps causing O(n-squared) cascade behavior. These are precisely the kinds of data-quality and algorithmic issues that produce false negatives in practice: if 66.7% of weight is lost due to pipeline ordering, the tool cannot possibly identify all bottlenecks.

### The Final Specification's Coverage Characterization

The final specification does provide building blocks for an honest characterization, though it never assembles them into an explicit statement:

- The `invariants_ok` flag (I-2 non-amplification ∧ I-7 locality) serves as a structural postcondition guard — it verifies that no edge's attributed delay exceeds its raw wait and that graph topology is preserved. (Note: I-1 conservation has been retired — per-entry-edge subtree-sum equality does not hold for fan-out graphs; see [ADR-016](ADR-016.md).) This is a necessary but not sufficient condition for algorithm correctness; known-bug detection relies on the full verification stack.
- The 50-microsecond false-wakeup filter explicitly trades completeness for noise reduction.
- The `MAX_CASCADE_DEPTH = 10` limit explicitly bounds recursion depth, meaning causal chains deeper than 10 levels are truncated.
- The `UNMATCHED_WAKEUP` metric tracks events that could not be correlated, providing a direct measure of data incompleteness.

These are the components of an honest coverage statement: "Under conditions of complete event capture (zero ring buffer drops, zero unmatched wakeups), with causal chain depth not exceeding 10 and no bottleneck consisting solely of sub-50-microsecond wait events, the algorithm is sound."

## Decision

Option B is chosen: the mathematical certainty claim is explicitly retracted and replaced with a pragmatic assurance.

The retraction is based on three converging lines of evidence:

1. **The claim was never defended.** Three specific breaking conditions were identified in the first technical review and ignored through two subsequent review cycles. A claim that cannot survive its first challenge is not a claim that should be maintained.

2. **The breaking conditions are not theoretical.** Ring buffer overflow, NTP-induced timestamp inversion, and user-space spinning are routine production phenomena, not edge cases. The archived project's own BPF code includes a drop counter, confirming that event loss occurs.

3. **Silent removal is worse than explicit retraction.** The claim disappeared from the final specification without acknowledgment. This leaves the historical record ambiguous and does not give users or developers a clear understanding of what the tool actually guarantees. Explicit retraction, with a precise replacement statement, is the professional engineering response.

The replacement guarantee takes the form: "wPerf enforces structural postconditions on its cascade algorithm (`invariants_ok` = I-2 non-amplification ∧ I-7 locality) and provides quantified observability of its own coverage gaps (ring buffer drop rate, unmatched wakeup count, partial stack ratio, cascade depth truncation count). The tool does not claim zero false negatives. Users should interpret results in the context of the reported coverage metrics." (See [ADR-016](ADR-016.md) for the retirement of I-1 conservation and the honest characterization of the sentinel's limitations.)

## Consequences

**Architecture:** The `output.json` schema must include explicit coverage metrics alongside the graph analysis results. The `graph_metrics` section already contains `invariants_ok` (renamed from `is_conserved` — see [ADR-016](ADR-016.md)); it must be extended with `drop_count`, `unmatched_wakeup_count`, `partial_stack_count`, `cascade_depth_truncation_count`, and `false_wakeup_filtered_count`. These metrics transform the tool from a black-box oracle ("trust me, no false negatives") into a transparent instrument ("here is what I found, and here is how complete my data was").

**Implementation:** The Cascade Engine's 6 invariants (I-2 non-amplification, I-3 non-negativity, I-4 termination, I-5 idempotency, I-6 depth monotonicity [simple chains only], I-7 locality) remain valid and must be enforced. I-1 (conservation) has been retired — see [ADR-016](ADR-016.md). I-6 is a qualified invariant that holds only for simple chains (no fan-out, no concurrent waiters). What changes is their interpretation: satisfying all 7 invariants proves that the algorithm is internally consistent, not that it has seen all relevant data. The property-based testing suite (10,000+ random graphs) validates algorithm correctness. The coverage metrics validate data completeness. Both are necessary; neither alone is sufficient.

**Risk:** The primary risk is that users may perceive the retraction as a weakness rather than an act of engineering integrity. This risk is mitigated by the fact that no competing tool (traditional off-CPU flame graphs, `perf sched`, lock profiling tools) makes a zero-false-negative claim either -- and none of them provide quantified coverage metrics. wPerf with explicit coverage metrics and honest characterization is more trustworthy than wPerf with an undefended mathematical certainty claim, because the former enables users to calibrate their confidence while the latter provides no recourse when the guarantee inevitably fails.

The retraction also has a positive engineering consequence: it redirects effort from defending an impossible guarantee toward minimizing and quantifying false negative probability. Investing in adaptive ring buffer sizing (reducing drop rate), NTP-aware timestamp correction (reducing correlation errors), and configurable filter thresholds (allowing users to trade noise tolerance for completeness) are all engineering improvements that become visible priorities once the false certainty is removed.
